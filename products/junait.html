Case Study Junait - 4 min read

I built a social network for kids - just to teach internet safety

Overview

Designed to look and function like a social network, Junait (pronounced “Unite) is an interactive platform teaching digital literacy and internet safety to elementary school kids in German-speaking regions.

It has been super rewarding to work with young kids, seeing their digital skills and confidence grow.

We stopped development and support 2017, and Junait is still growing with an average of 60 percent YoY!

Why I created this product

Problem and Target Audience: Too Many Kids Are Vulnerable to Internet Threats

Approached by strangers, installing adware, or falling for clickbait - the internet is full of threats and dark patterns. Especially young kids are easy targets, since they lack the skills to distinguish between what’s genuine and misleading. Teachers want to help, but just talking to their students doesn't quite hit the mark.

I defined Junait’s Value Proposition

We offer a tool to teachers that allows young kids to dip their toes into a social media-like environment, making it a social experience to learn about the perils of being online.

Thinking outside the box

Junait functions like a fully-fledged social network, creating a magical atmosphere in the classroom. After the kids create their own avatars and profile pages, they discover they can chat and interact with each other. Such a multiplayer environment is rare in educational settings and a delight to observe. Note: The social network is NOT public. The teacher creates a private instance for their class.

All lessons are tied together by an engaging narrative: The evil Dr. Virus has infected the network, and the students must assist Condor, the cool hacker girl, in stopping him. The network acts like a haunted house, filled with pre-programmed dangers like bots and viruses, designed to ensnare and deceive the students.

Every mistake and progress made by the students is converted into live data, visually presented to the teacher in real time. There's always much laughter and lively discussion at the end of the class. When these results are shown to the kids it helps them reflect on their firsthand experiences.
